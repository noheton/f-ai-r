\section{The F(AI)\texorpdfstring{\textsuperscript{2}}{2}R Pattern}
\label{sec:pattern}

This section states the pattern in full. \S\ref{sec:axes} defines the
axes. \S\ref{sec:pipeline} describes the ten-stage agent pipeline.
\S\ref{sec:ladder} defines the verification ladder as a finite-state
machine and crosswalks it to PRISMA 2020 and GRADE.
\S\ref{sec:handback} specifies the handback discipline, and
\S\ref{sec:mirror} the mirror discipline. The eight integrated
practices that animate the pattern are deferred to Section~\ref{sec:eight}.

\subsection{Axes: F(AI)\texorpdfstring{\textsuperscript{2}}{2}R}
\label{sec:axes}

We retain the four canonical FAIR axes and read each in two passes.
The authoring pass (AI\textsubscript{1}) is the agent or human who
\emph{produces} the artefact for that axis. The audit pass
(AI\textsubscript{2}) is the agent or human who \emph{verifies} the
artefact against the axis after the fact, never in the same role and
never in the same commit. The two passes are what justifies the
``squared'' notation. The (AI) factor is multiplied through every
axis: there is an authoring and an audit pass for Findability, for
Accessibility, for Interoperability, and for Reusability.

\paragraph{Findable, with (AI).}
The authoring pass mints persistent identifiers
(DOI via Zenodo; ORCID for authors), populates machine-readable metadata
(\texttt{CITATION.cff}, \texttt{codemeta.json},
\texttt{.zenodo.json}), and assigns IRIs to per-claim entities in the
provenance graph. The audit pass verifies that the four metadata
surfaces agree, that no IRI is dangling, and that every claim
referenced in the manuscript has a graph entry.

\paragraph{Accessible, with (AI).}
The authoring pass clones-and-builds: a third party with the public
repository and a documented toolchain can reproduce the PDF. The
audit pass verifies the licence pair (MIT for code/prompts/RDF,
CC-BY-4.0 for prose), checks for paywalled sources that should have
been vendored, and confirms that the structured redaction policy
(Appendix~\ref{app:redaction}) covers what it claims to cover.

\paragraph{Interoperable, with (AI).}
The authoring pass commits the provenance graph as PROV-O over Turtle
in \texttt{doc/provenance.ttl}, with stable namespaces and the
\texttt{fair2r:} subclasses listed in \S\ref{sec:bg-prov}. The audit
pass validates the Turtle, runs SHACL shapes if defined, and verifies
that every section of the manuscript has a \texttt{prov:Activity}
record that produced it.

\paragraph{Reusable, with (AI).}
The authoring pass commits the per-role prompts under \texttt{agents/},
the build target under \texttt{paper/Makefile}, and the day-to-day
logbook under \texttt{doc/logbook.md}. The audit pass replays the
pipeline on a clean clone, compares the output to the published
artefact, and reports drift.

The crucial property is that the \emph{same agent never performs both
passes}. We return to this in \S\ref{sec:handback}.

\subsection{The pipeline as the team}
\label{sec:pipeline}

The pipeline is the team; the team is the methodology. Ten stages,
coordinated by an Orchestrator, with a strict handback discipline
between prose-owning agents and audit-owning agents:

\begin{description}
    \item[0. Orchestrator.] Inspects state, applies a dispatch table,
        routes work to \emph{exactly one} downstream stage. Never
        edits prose. Only agent permitted to launch other agents.
    \item[1. Research.] Per-section literature audit. Produces sources,
        transcripts, and provenance maps under
        \texttt{doc/sources/} and \texttt{doc/transcripts/}.
    \item[1.5. Source Analyzer.] Upgrades retrieved sources from
        \textsc{lit-retrieved} to \textsc{ai-confirmed}; never crosses
        into \textsc{lit-read}. Introduced specifically to attack the
        verification-overhead bottleneck described in
        \S\ref{sec:ladder}.
    \item[2. Scientific Writer.] Owns prose, register, and \LaTeX{}.
        Does \emph{not} change claims, modify evidence, or introduce
        citations without a verified source.
    \item[3. Illustration.] Owns figures. Consumes
        \texttt{[ILLUSTRATION OPPORTUNITY]} markers from the writer.
        Prefers \textsc{ai-confirmed} or \textsc{lit-read} data; halts
        on load-bearing values that are still \textsc{lit-retrieved}.
    \item[4. Layout Scrutinizer.] Reads the \emph{rendered PDF} and
        files a Layout Defect Registry under
        \texttt{doc/handbacks/layout-*.md}. Defects are not fixed in
        place by this agent.
    \item[5. Readability \& Novelty Scrutinizer.] Reads the markdown
        front-to-back and flags repetition, list-of-lists prose,
        hedging chains, and unsupported novelty against
        \texttt{doc/sources.md}. Files a Readability Defect Registry.
    \item[6. Aligner (FAIR).] Audits traceability across paper, sources,
        \texttt{README.md}, and the handback registries. Routes
        per-defect hand-backs; never edits content.
    \item[7. Modeler (Provenance Curator).] Renders the traceability
        spine into a W3C PROV-O graph
        (\texttt{doc/provenance.ttl}). Refuses to invent triples.
    \item[8. Site Agent.] Owns the public-facing site under
        \texttt{site/} and \texttt{\_site/}. Refuses to render without
        explicit publication consent. The site never introduces a new
        claim.
\end{description}

A separate \emph{Condenser} stage produces the \(\le\)10-page core
submission, mirroring the long form into
\texttt{paper/main-condensed.tex} under independent application of the
mirror discipline (\S\ref{sec:mirror}).

The strict separation is a structural property. The agent that owns
prose cannot mark its own claims as verified. The agent that audits
layout cannot edit prose. The agent that curates provenance cannot
write \LaTeX. \emph{Specialisation reduces hallucination at the seam}:
each agent's input is checkable by the previous stage, each agent's
output is checkable by the next.

\subsection{The verification ladder as a finite-state machine}
\label{sec:ladder}

Every claim in the manuscript carries a verification state. The states
are organised as a finite-state machine, monotone non-decreasing modulo
explicit retraction:

\begin{quote}
\textsc{unverified-external}
\(\to\) \textsc{needs-research}
\(\to\) \textsc{lit-retrieved}
\(\to\) \textsc{ai-confirmed}
\(\to\) \textsc{lit-read}
\end{quote}

The semantics:

\begin{description}
    \item[\textsc{unverified-external}.] The claim originates outside
        this repository (a model's prior knowledge, a casual reference)
        and has not been checked.
    \item[\textsc{needs-research}.] The claim has been recognised as
        needing a source; the search is queued under
        \texttt{doc/research-protocol.md}.
    \item[\textsc{lit-retrieved}.] A candidate source has been
        retrieved (URL or DOI), but no agent has read it.
    \item[\textsc{ai-confirmed}.] An AI agent (the Source Analyzer)
        has read the retrieved source and confirmed that it supports
        the claim, with a quoted snippet preserved in
        \texttt{doc/sources.md}.
    \item[\textsc{lit-read}.] A human author has read the source and
        confirmed both that it supports the claim and that the
        inference from source to claim is sound. Equivalent for
        graph-internal purposes to \textsc{source-vendored}.
\end{description}

A claim may invoke a source at its current rung or below. Load-bearing
or contested claims still require \textsc{lit-read}.

The \textsc{ai-confirmed} rung was introduced after observing that
verification overhead is real and dominates writer cycles. Most
retrieved entries are obvious-from-the-abstract; a small minority
anchor contested claims; the single-tier gate forced the human reader
through both populations at the same cost. Splitting the work between
the Source Analyzer (cheap) and the human (expensive) is the lever
that makes the practice economically tenable.

\paragraph{Crosswalk to PRISMA 2020 and GRADE.}
The ladder admits a useful, qualified mapping. The retrieval-depth axis
maps onto PRISMA 2020: \textsc{lit-retrieved} corresponds to
Identification, \textsc{ai-confirmed} to Screening,
\textsc{lit-read} to Included. The invocation-strength axis maps onto
GRADE: a claim invoked at \textsc{lit-read} carries a GRADE certainty
of \textsc{moderate} or \textsc{high} for the inference from source to
claim, while a claim invoked at \textsc{ai-confirmed} can carry at
most \textsc{low}. GRADE certainty is a rating of the underlying
evidence, not of the retrieval procedure; the mapping above is the
floor the rung permits, not a guarantee of the ceiling.

\subsection{Handback discipline}
\label{sec:handback}

The four scrutinizing stages --- Source Analyzer, Layout Scrutinizer,
Readability \& Novelty Scrutinizer, Aligner --- never edit source.
They write \emph{Defect Registries} under \texttt{doc/handbacks/},
each entry naming the file, the line, the defect, and the proposed
remedy. The repair is performed by the appropriate prose-owning
agent (Scientific Writer, Illustration, or the human author) in a
subsequent commit.

The cost is one extra commit boundary per cycle. The payoff is that
``what did the scrutinizer actually flag?'' has a single canonical
answer in the git history, and that the scrutinizer's incentive to
make their objection survive review is aligned with the writer's
incentive to address it cleanly. A scrutinizer that may patch in place
is, at the limit, a writer with a different hat; an audit that produces
a registry is an audit that can be reasoned about.

\subsection{Mirror discipline}
\label{sec:mirror}

Every primary artefact has two mirrored representations: the
machine-readable source under version control, and the rendered output
that humans read. The pair is enforced for the manuscript
(markdown notes \(\leftrightarrow\) \LaTeX{} sources
\(\leftrightarrow\) PDF), for the provenance graph
(Turtle \(\leftrightarrow\) the rendered tables on the public site),
for the prompts (markdown source under \texttt{agents/}
\(\leftrightarrow\) HTML on the site), and for the logbook
(markdown source \(\leftrightarrow\) HTML, plus the corresponding
PROV-O activity records).

The discipline buys two things. First, drift between source and render
is detectable (the build either succeeds or fails; the rendered tables
either match the Turtle or do not). Second, an audit pass can compare
mirrors --- a claim that survived condensation, for example, must keep
its \texttt{fair2r:Claim} IRI in both the long and condensed manuscripts
(\S\ref{sec:practice-mirror}).

The pattern, stated in one sentence: \emph{the manuscript, the
provenance graph, and the logbook are primary artefacts and must remain
consistent and up to date at all times}. A commit that updates one of
them and leaves the others lagging is a defect, even if the build
passes.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

FAIR~\cite{wilkinson2016fair} is the substrate;
FAIR4RS~\cite{chuehong2022fair4rs} and
FAIR4ML~\cite{ravi2024fair4ml} are the prior re-readings.
F(AI)\textsuperscript{2}R is the third such extension and the first
that targets artefacts generated downstream of the writing process
itself. Datasheets for datasets~\cite{gebru2021datasheets} and model
cards~\cite{mitchell2019modelcards} are the canonical attempts to
attach human-readable, structured documentation to ML artefacts and
are the immediate ancestors of the per-prompt and per-claim
documentation we propose; they document an artefact at a single
level, while F(AI)\textsuperscript{2}R documents the artefact and
the process that produced it, chained.

PROV-O~\cite{w3c2013provo} is the formal substrate. The recent
ML-reproducibility literature has produced reproducibility checklists
and executable-paper systems; F(AI)\textsuperscript{2}R differs in
that the writing layer is itself the artefact under reproducibility
audit. PRISMA 2020~\cite{page2021prisma} and
GRADE~\cite{guyatt2008grade} supply the retrieval-depth and
invocation-strength axes the verification ladder crosswalks.

The \textsc{ICMJE}~\cite{icmje2023}, ACL Rolling Review, NeurIPS, and
the major scientific publishers have converged on disclosure being
mandatory and AI authorship being unacceptable. The literature on
what \emph{adequate} disclosure looks like is thinner; Bender and
colleagues~\cite{bender2021stochastic} provide much of its
vocabulary. The sixth integrated practice
(\S\ref{sec:eight}, item~6) is our attempt to operationalise
adequate disclosure in a single submission. The base-rate
literature on
fabrication~\cite{walters2023fabrication,magesh2024legal} is the
load-bearing empirical input to the verification ladder. The source
repository \texttt{noheton/Obscurity-Is-Dead}~\cite{krebs2026obscurity}
is the immediate methodological ancestor of this paper; this paper
is its abstraction.

\paragraph{Formal methods: model checking and theorem proving.}
Read structurally, F(AI)\textsuperscript{2}R borrows more from
formal methods than the related-work neighbours above. Each
\texttt{fair2r:Claim} IRI is a conjecture; the verification ladder
is a proof-state machine; the audit pass is a proof-checker that
verifies structural properties of the graph rather than
re-discovering them; the SHACL shapes (\S\ref{sec:domain-ontologies})
are invariants; the handback discipline is the prover / checker
separation that model checking and certified-software efforts
treat as load-bearing
\cite{clarke2009modelchecking,klein2009sel4}\todo[inline]{verify}.
The analogy is partial --- a manuscript is not a transition system,
and \textsc{lit-read} is not soundness --- but it is closer than
the analogy to data-management standards, and it suggests two
useful imports we have not yet drawn on: (i) \emph{counter-example
witnesses} attached to retracted claims, so a future reader sees
\emph{why} a claim was invalidated, not only \emph{that} it was;
(ii) \emph{lemma libraries} of vetted claims that successor papers
can cite by IRI without re-verifying. We name these as future work.

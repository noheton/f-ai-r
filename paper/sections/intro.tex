\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The FAIR Guiding Principles for scientific data
management~\cite{wilkinson2016fair} were drafted for data, then
extended to research software (FAIR4RS~\cite{chuehong2022fair4rs}) and
to machine-learning models (FAIR4ML~\cite{ravi2024fair4ml}). Neither
extension yet covers the artefact classes that LLM-assisted scholarly
writing produces: exportable conversation transcripts, versioned
prompt files, tool-and-model version manifests, verification-status
ladders, structured redaction policies, and per-claim provenance maps.

This is not a hypothetical gap. Anyone now writing a paper with an
LLM in the room is producing those artefacts, whether or not they
preserve them. If they vanish, two things follow. First, the audit
trail is gone --- a reader cannot ask \emph{who wrote this sentence,
what evidence supports this claim, and how was the claim
verified?}~\cite{krebs2026obscurity}. Second, the work is more
exposed to the documented failure modes of LLM-assisted writing:
fabricated citations, drift between claim and evidence,
sloppification, and the gradual flattening of an argument as hedges
accrete. Reported base rates are not anecdotal: Walters and
Wilder~\cite{walters2023fabrication} report 55\% fabrication for
GPT-3.5 and 18\% for GPT-4 across general-purpose generation;
Magesh et al.~\cite{magesh2024legal} report 17--34\% hallucination
for retrieval-augmented legal-research tools. The numbers are not
directly comparable --- one grades full citations, the other grades
RAG-anchored answers --- but they are the same order of magnitude,
and it is the kind of number that turns a methodological
inconvenience into a disqualifying one.

The volume problem compounds the per-paper one. The fraction of
journal and arXiv submissions that contain LLM-generated or
LLM-assisted prose has risen sharply since
2023~\cite{liang2024mapping,vannoorden2023chatgpt}; detection studies
report measurable increases across computer science, biomedicine, and
the social sciences~\cite{kobak2024delving}. Editors at major venues
have publicly described AI-shaped text as a load they cannot review
out of submissions one paragraph at a time, and have asked for
provenance \emph{from} the authors instead of post-hoc detection
\emph{against} them~\cite{else2023chatgpt,thorp2023chatgpt}\todo[inline]{verify}. Paper mills, fabricated
literatures, and the boilerplate flattening of disclosure pages are
the symptoms; what is missing is a discipline that authors can adopt
on day one rather than a forensic regime that reviewers apply on the
back end. The pattern this paper proposes is meant to be that
discipline.

We propose \emph{F(AI)\textsuperscript{2}R}, read \emph{F-A-I-A-I-R}:
the canonical FAIR axes with an additional \emph{(AI)} factor
multiplied through them, squared because each axis demands two passes
over every artefact, an authoring pass and an audit pass. An earlier
draft used \emph{FAIR4AI}; we discarded it after observing that
\emph{4AI heißt alles} --- a suffix denotes nothing about how the
principles change. Folding \emph{(AI)} into the acronym, multiplied
through, asserts the extension is internal to FAIR rather than
external to it.

\paragraph{Contribution.} The contribution is not the name. It is the
integration of eight individually unoriginal practices into a single
discipline: (1) transcript preservation; (2) verification-status
labelling; (3) per-claim provenance maps; (4) mirror discipline
between source and render; (5) recursive meta-process documentation;
(6) base-rate-anchored AI disclosure; (7) legal honesty about
authorship; and (8) FAIR alignment treated as a precondition, never
as a retrofit. None of the eight is novel on its own. Their joint
adoption, enforced by a ten-stage agent pipeline with strict
separation between prose-owning and audit-owning agents, is what we
claim is new. \emph{The pipeline is the team; the team is the
methodology.}

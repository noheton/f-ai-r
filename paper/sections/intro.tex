\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The FAIR Guiding Principles for scientific data management were published
in 2016 and have, over the intervening decade, become the default
language in which the research-data community talks about
quality~\cite{wilkinson2016fair}. They were drafted for data. The
community subsequently extended them to research
software~\cite{chuehong2022fair4rs} and to machine-learning
models~\cite{ravi2024fair4ml}. Both extensions are pragmatic
re-readings of the original four axes for a different artefact class.
Neither yet covers the artefact classes that have appeared since
generative AI began participating in scholarly production: exportable
conversation transcripts, versioned prompt files, tool-and-model version
manifests, verification-status ladders, structured redaction policies,
and per-claim provenance maps.

This is not a hypothetical gap. Anyone now writing a paper with an LLM
in the room is producing those artefacts, whether or not they preserve
them. The question is whether they travel with the manuscript or vanish
the moment the chat window closes. If they vanish, two things follow.
First, the audit trail is gone --- a reader cannot ask
\emph{who wrote this sentence, what evidence supports this claim, and
how was the claim verified?}~\cite{krebs2026obscurity} Second, the work
is more vulnerable to the well-documented failure modes of
LLM-assisted writing: fabricated citations, drift between claim and
evidence, sloppification, and the gradual flattening of an argument as
hedges accrete. Reported base rates for fabricated citations in LLM
output range from roughly 18\% to 55\% depending on
domain~\cite{walters2023fabrication,magesh2024legal}, which is the kind
of number that turns a methodological inconvenience into a
disqualifying one.

We propose \emph{F(AI)\textsuperscript{2}R}, read \emph{F-A-I-A-I-R}.
The name is not a new principle. It is a typographic commitment: the
canonical FAIR axes with an additional \emph{(AI)} factor multiplied
through them, squared because each axis demands two passes over every
artefact --- an authoring pass and an audit pass. The original draft of
this work used the name \emph{FAIR4AI}; we discarded it after observing
that \emph{4AI heißt alles} (\emph{4AI means everything}): a suffix
denotes nothing about how the principles change. Folding \emph{(AI)}
into the acronym, multiplied through, asserts that the extension is
internal to FAIR rather than external to it.

The contribution of this paper is not the name. The contribution is the
integration of eight individually unoriginal practices into a single
discipline. The eight are: (1) transcript preservation, (2)
verification-status labelling, (3) per-claim provenance maps, (4)
mirror discipline between markdown source and \LaTeX{} render, (5)
recursive meta-process documentation (the paper is its own case
study), (6) base-rate-anchored AI disclosure, (7) legal honesty about
authorship, and (8) FAIR alignment treated as a precondition rather
than a retrofit. None of the eight is novel on its own. None taken
alone resists more than a subset of the failure modes the literature
has documented. Their joint adoption, enforced by a ten-stage agent
pipeline with strict separation between prose-owning and audit-owning
agents, is what we claim is new.

The pipeline is the team; the team is the methodology. Specialisation
reduces hallucination at the seam: an agent that owns prose is not
trusted to assess its own claims, and an agent that audits claims is
not trusted to rewrite the prose. The cost is one extra commit boundary
per cycle. The payoff is that ``what did the scrutinizer actually
flag?'' has a single canonical answer in the git history.

\paragraph{Roadmap.}
Section~\ref{sec:background} reviews FAIR, its existing extensions,
PROV-O, PRISMA 2020, GRADE, and the AI-disclosure norms relevant to the
discussion. Section~\ref{sec:pattern} states the F(AI)\textsuperscript{2}R
pattern in full: the renamed axes, the ten-stage pipeline, the
verification ladder as a finite-state machine, the handback discipline,
and the mirror discipline. Section~\ref{sec:eight} treats the eight
integrated practices in turn, each with the failure mode it addresses
and the artefact it produces. Section~\ref{sec:impl} grounds the
pattern in the repository that produced this paper.
Section~\ref{sec:failure-modes} names the failure modes the pattern is
meant to resist and is honest about which of them it does not.
Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses the dual-use surface, the
limitations of a single worked example, and the conditions under which
we will submit F(AI)\textsuperscript{2}R to RDA / FORCE11 as a
candidate community recommendation.

\paragraph{What this paper is not.}
It is not a survey of LLM-assisted writing tools. It is not a
prescription for any one model, harness, or vendor. It is not an
ethical defence of AI-assisted scientific work in general; that
defence has to be made artefact by artefact, and naming what we are
already doing is the first step toward surrendering it to peer
scrutiny.

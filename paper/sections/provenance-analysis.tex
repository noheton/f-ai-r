\section{Provenance Analysis}
\label{sec:provenance-analysis}

The graph at \texttt{doc/provenance.ttl} is not a passive record;
it is a queryable structure whose health \emph{is} the audit pass
on \textbf{Reusable} (\S\ref{sec:axes}).
Figure~\ref{fig:provenance-topology} renders the schema with the
current instance counts read at compile time; the populated
verification ladder is on the conference poster
(\texttt{slides/poster-A0.tex}). The four diagnostic SPARQL queries the
audit reads --- rung distribution, section-to-claim coverage,
source invocation strength, activity timeline --- and the
auto-generated tables they produce are walked in
Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix-provenance-analysis}.

\begin{figure}[!h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.96\linewidth]{figures/provenance-topology.pdf}

Five PROV-O lanes (Agents, Plans, Activities, Entities, Claims)

The verification ladder rendered as a finite-state machine with
live rung populations is on the conference poster
(\texttt{slides/poster-A0.tex}); the queries that produce those
populations are walked in
Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix-provenance-analysis}.

The four queries are diagnostic, not exhaustive. They surface the
failure modes of \S\ref{sec:failure-modes} that have a graph
signature --- ghost citations, drift between prose and graph,
sections without curated claims --- and explicitly do not surface
\textbf{semantic} drift (a claim correctly recorded in the graph
whose prose has shifted away from its evidence). That is the
readability scrutinizer's job (\S\ref{sec:pipeline}). A live
interactive view of the graph is published at
\href{https://noheton.github.io/f-ai-r/provenance.html}{the
\textbf{Provenance} tab of the public site}.
